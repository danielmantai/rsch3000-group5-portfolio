Chapter 01

Research Question & Rationale

Why we focused on coordinated, identity-accessible services in Edmonton.

Research question

How might we improve access to essential services for individuals experiencing homelessness in Edmonton through a more coordinated and identity-accessible support system?

Rationale

Our team selected this question after identifying recurring barriers faced by individuals experiencing homelessness in Edmonton. Through systems mapping and stakeholder interviews, we found that fragmented services, identification requirements, and repeated intake processes often prevent individuals from accessing timely support. Improving access to services can reduce frustration, improve continuity of care, and create stronger pathways toward stable housing.

Our leverage analysis identified the loss and absence of identification documents as the most concrete, addressable barrier inside a broader pattern of service fragmentation. Matt, a former frontline worker at Hope Mission Edmonton, described a landscape where "each organization does specific good work but operates in isolation" (Matt, personal communication, March 12, 2026). His observation that photo identification is "Ground Zero" for service access reframed the entire problem for our group.

Leverage areas identified

High leverage (mental models and system goals). Housing treated as a commodity rather than a human right shapes every policy and funding decision downstream. Addiction framed as a moral failure rather than a health condition determines who gets funded to respond and how. Measuring success by shelter beds filled rather than people housed permanently means the system optimizes for the wrong outcome.

Medium leverage (information flows and system structure). Services are fragmented across dozens of organizations, each with its own intake process. People rely on word-of-mouth and trial and error. Within this fragmentation, identification requirements create a hard barrier at the entry point of nearly every service beyond emergency shelter.

Low leverage (parameters). Adding shelter beds, adjusting operating hours, and expanding intake policies produce marginal improvements. These are the most visible interventions and the least effective at scale.

Stakeholder interest

The assignment overview names three non-academic audiences who have expressed interest in this work: MLA housing opposition, the Mayor's office, and the frontline workers we interviewed, including Matt. The research question is framed so that a policy response, a municipal coordination response, and a frontline-worker response are all legible endpoints.
