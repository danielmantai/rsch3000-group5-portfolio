Chapter 03

Literature Review

Evidence-based framing of identification barriers, service fragmentation, and housing-first approaches.

What the literature shows

Research consistently shows that individuals experiencing homelessness face structural barriers when accessing services. Lack of government-issued identification can restrict access to housing supports, employment programs, healthcare, and income assistance (Homeless Hub, 2017; Government of Alberta, n.d.). Many support systems operate independently, creating fragmented pathways that require individuals to repeatedly restart intake processes.

Studies on coordinated support models and Housing First approaches indicate that stable housing and integrated supports improve outcomes (Gaetz et al., 2013). Identity and documentation barriers remain under-addressed within this framework. Available programs exist, yet individuals cannot access them efficiently. Funding accountability rules tied to documented service delivery reinforce the gap (Housing, Infrastructure and Communities Canada, n.d.).

ESN Connect was developed in response to this gap by creating identity through verified service engagement rather than requiring formal identification upfront. The design is grounded in a systemic-design framing of where leverage actually sits in a service system (Ryan, 2013; Meadows, 2001).
