Final Proposal

The pitch: what we are proposing, why it matters systemically, and what has to change for ESN Connect to move from prototype to structural intervention.

Systemic impact

Our iceberg analysis made the structural nature of the problem visible. At the surface: encampments and shelter overcrowding. Below that: patterns of people cycling through services, losing identification, starting over. Deeper still: fragmented organizations, funding tied to bed counts instead of outcomes, and identification requirements embedded in funding accountability rules.

At the bottom we identified "housing as a commodity" as the mental model holding the structure in place. Policies, programs, and funding formulas all rest on that assumption. The leverage point is shifting that assumption toward "housing is a right," because doing so ripples through every layer above it. Rules change, goals shift, programs get redesigned. You cannot find that from the event level alone; you need the iceberg.

ESN Connect does not try to change the mental model by argument. It changes the information flow. When shelter-issued identity is accepted as the basis for a funded service record, the system has to reckon with people who were previously invisible to it. That is a structural shift, not a programmatic one.

Trade-offs

The proposal makes three deliberate trade-offs.

Worker-facing, not client-facing. A client app assumes a phone, charge, data, and stability. A worker portal assumes a trained staff member at intake. The worker is the trusted interface. The cost is that the system depends on sustained staff training across participating organizations.

Network-issued identity, not government-issued. ESN Connect creates identity through verified service engagement rather than waiting for the government ID process. The cost is that the credential is only as useful as the number of organizations that accept it. Network effects matter; the first few organizations carry disproportionate risk.

Shared records, not siloed records. Cross-organization lookup cuts duplication and preserves continuity. The cost is data governance: if privacy protections fail, the breach hits every participating organization at once. Client control over who sees the record is a requirement, not a feature.

Stakeholder perspectives

MLA housing opposition

The proposal gives opposition housing critics a concrete reform to point at: a network-issued credential that Service Alberta can recognize for the narrow purpose of service access, without requiring a full rewrite of the provincial ID framework. Low legislative cost, visible frontline impact.

Mayor's office

For municipal coordination, ESN Connect reduces the duplication that makes shelter operations expensive and uneven. Shared records cut intake churn. Documented service records satisfy Reaching Home funding requirements, making the people who are currently invisible to funders visible, which unlocks resourcing for the hardest cases.

Frontline workers (Matt McMechan)

Matt framed photo ID as "Ground Zero" for service access. The proposal is a direct response to that framing. Workers at any participating shelter or outreach team can issue a card at first contact and carry the record forward. Less retelling, less re-proving, more time on the actual help.

Future directions

Two reinforcing loops drive growth. First, as more organizations adopt the system, more service records are created, which strengthens funding applications, which attracts more organizations. Second, as individuals build continuous service records, transitions to stable housing speed up, freeing shelter capacity for new clients.

The longer-term goal is not a permanent parallel ID system. Success means ESN Connect demonstrates enough value that policy-makers simplify the government ID process for people in shelters. The card becomes a bridge, not a destination.

What happens next

The portfolio and prototype are the starting point. The next step is a structured conversation with Homeward Trust Edmonton about governance, with Service Alberta about credential recognition, and with two or three shelter operators willing to pilot the card issuance workflow. The work after that is policy: making shelter-issued identity a legitimate basis for funded service delivery, not a workaround.
