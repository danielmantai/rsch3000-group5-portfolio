Chapter 06

Final Proposal

The pitch: what we are proposing, why it matters systemically, and what has to change for ESN Connect to move from prototype to structural intervention.

Opening

The barrier that traps people without ID in Edmonton's homelessness service system is not a matter of willingness. Frontline workers at shelters, food banks, clinics, and housing programs want to help everyone who walks through the door. The obstacle is structural: government funding for social services requires documented proof that an identifiable person was served, and that proof hinges on ID many unhoused people genuinely cannot obtain. ESN Connect is Group 5's proposal for a shelter-issued credential, backed by a shared digital record, that lets frontline workers vouch for identity and keep a person visible to the system that is supposed to help them.

Human impact

Before this becomes a policy conversation, it is a personal one. For a person experiencing homelessness, lacking identification can mean being turned away from housing opportunities, delayed from receiving healthcare, or having to repeat their story to a new worker every time they seek help. What appears to be a missing document often becomes weeks or months of lost momentum. ESN Connect is designed to reduce that friction by making the first point of contact count, preserving progress instead of forcing people to start over.

Every system change described in the rest of this proposal is in service of that experience. The funding rules, the credential, the shared record, the pilot, and the policy work all matter only because they shorten the distance between the person at the door and the help they came for.

Systemic impact

Our iceberg analysis made the structural nature of the problem visible. At the surface: encampments and shelter overcrowding. Below that: patterns of people cycling through services, losing identification, starting over. Deeper still: fragmented organizations, funding tied to bed counts instead of outcomes, and identification requirements embedded in funding accountability rules.

At the bottom we identified "housing as a commodity" as the mental model holding the structure in place. Policies, programs, and funding formulas all rest on that assumption. The leverage point is shifting that assumption toward "housing is a right," because doing so ripples through every layer above it. Rules change, goals shift, programs get redesigned. You cannot find that from the event level alone; you need the iceberg.

ESN Connect does not try to change the mental model by argument. It changes the information flow. When shelter-issued identity is accepted as the basis for a funded service record, the system has to reckon with people who were previously invisible to it. That is a structural shift, not a programmatic one.

Before and after ESN Connect

The same person, the same network of services, two very different trajectories.

Before ESN Connect

Individuals move between shelters and services with no portable identity record. Intake is repeated, documents are lost, and support workers have limited visibility into prior progress. People most in need can remain invisible to the systems meant to help them.

After ESN Connect

Individuals receive a trusted service credential and continuity of record across participating organizations. Intake becomes faster, referrals become smoother, and verified service engagement becomes visible for funding and case coordination. Progress can follow the person instead of being tied to one location.

Trade-offs

The proposal makes three deliberate trade-offs.

Worker-facing, not client-facing. A client app assumes a phone, charge, data, and stability. A worker portal assumes a trained staff member at intake. The worker is the trusted interface. The cost is that the system depends on sustained staff training across participating organizations.

Network-issued identity, not government-issued. ESN Connect creates identity through verified service engagement rather than waiting for the government ID process. The cost is that the credential is only as useful as the number of organizations that accept it. Network effects matter; the first few organizations carry disproportionate risk.

Shared records, not siloed records. Cross-organization lookup cuts duplication and preserves continuity. The cost is data governance: if privacy protections fail, the breach hits every participating organization at once. Client control over who sees the record is a requirement, not a feature.

Stakeholder perspectives

MLA housing opposition

The proposal gives opposition housing critics a concrete reform to point at: a network-issued credential that Service Alberta can recognize for the narrow purpose of service access, without requiring a full rewrite of the provincial ID framework. Low legislative cost, visible frontline impact.

Mayor's office

For municipal coordination, ESN Connect reduces the duplication that makes shelter operations expensive and uneven. Shared records cut intake churn. Documented service records satisfy Reaching Home funding requirements, making the people who are currently invisible to funders visible, which unlocks resourcing for the hardest cases.

Frontline workers (Matt)

Matt framed photo ID as "Ground Zero" for service access. The proposal is a direct response to that framing. Workers at any participating shelter or outreach team can issue a card at first contact and carry the record forward. Less retelling, less re-proving, more time on the actual help.

Recommended pilot

The next practical step is a six-month pilot involving two Edmonton shelter operators, one outreach team, and a coordinating partner such as Homeward Trust Edmonton. The pilot would test frontline card issuance, shared records governance, client consent processes, and measurable outcomes such as reduced intake duplication, faster referrals, and improved continuity of care.

Six months is long enough to surface real workflow friction inside participating organizations and short enough to keep the scope honest. The success criteria are deliberately concrete: intake time per returning client, percentage of cross-organization referrals that arrive with a complete record, and the share of pilot participants who reach a stable service relationship within the pilot window. None of those measures require new instrumentation; they sit on top of data the participating organizations already collect.

The point of the pilot is not to prove the prototype. It is to learn what governance, training, and policy changes the network would need before scaling beyond the first two operators.

Future directions

Two reinforcing loops drive growth. First, as more organizations adopt the system, more service records are created, which strengthens funding applications, which attracts more organizations. Second, as individuals build continuous service records, transitions to stable housing speed up, freeing shelter capacity for new clients.

The longer-term goal is a temporary scaffold. Success means ESN Connect demonstrates enough value that policy-makers simplify the government ID process for people in shelters. The card becomes a bridge that can eventually be folded back into the government system.

What happens next

The portfolio and prototype are the starting point. Three near-term conversations move the work from artifact to commitment: Homeward Trust Edmonton on governance of a shared records framework, Service Alberta on recognition of a network-issued credential for the narrow purpose of service access, and two or three shelter operators willing to host the pilot described above. Each of those conversations can begin in the next quarter without waiting for legislative change.

The work after that is policy: making shelter-issued identity a legitimate basis for funded service delivery inside the system rather than an informal workaround beside it. Building this website took weeks. Changing the funding model will take years of policy work, coalition building, and advocacy. That gap is the reason to start making the argument clearly now, with a concrete demonstration of what the solution could look like.

Final ask

ESN Connect is not a finished policy. It is a practical first step toward a system where access to help is not blocked by the absence of identification. Group 5 is asking policy-makers, funders, and frontline organizations to treat this prototype as the beginning of a serious conversation about structural change.

Concretely: read the prototype walkthrough, share this portfolio with a colleague who works on housing or shelter policy, and tell us what would have to be true for your organization to participate in a six-month pilot. The barrier we have described is structural, but the first move out of it is a conversation, and we are asking for that conversation now.
