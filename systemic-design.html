Chapter 02

Systemic Design Artifacts

A selection of the diagrams and tools our group used to move from events to patterns, structures, and mental models.

How we used systemic design

The iceberg model, causal loop diagram, and empathy map each gave us a different lens on the same problem. Together they moved the group from visible events (encampments, shelter overcrowding) down through patterns, structures, and mental models. The Gold Mine Matrix then translated that analysis into a ranked set of candidate interventions and pointed us at the prototype direction. The artifacts below are a curated selection from a larger body of group work, each presented as a high-resolution image followed by a short walkthrough of what it surfaced.

Iceberg model walkthrough

The iceberg helped our team move beyond visible events such as shelter overcrowding and encampments to examine deeper patterns, structures, and mental models influencing homelessness in Edmonton. At the surface, we mapped the events most residents see: encampments in the river valley, shelter lineups, hospital emergency department pressure. One layer down, patterns: people cycling repeatedly through emergency services, losing identification, restarting intake from zero. Deeper still, structures: fragmented organizations, funding tied to bed counts rather than outcomes, and identification requirements embedded in funding accountability rules.

At the base of the iceberg we identified "housing as a commodity" as the mental model holding the rest of the structure in place. Policies, programs, and funding formulas all rest on that assumption. Shifting toward "housing as a right" would ripple through every layer above it.

Causal loop walkthrough

Our group developed three iterations of the causal loop diagram as the analysis sharpened. Each version focused on a different entry point: the ID-replacement trap, the funding-invisibility loop, and the displacement cycle. The final shared reading pulled from all three.

The strongest reinforcing loop runs through identification. A person loses ID. They cannot re-enter a funded program without it. They cannot access housing supports without the program. They cannot establish a stable mailing address without housing. They cannot replace ID without an address. The loop closes on itself.

A second reinforcing loop runs through funding. When shelters cannot document service delivery for people without ID, those individuals become invisible to funders. Funding shifts toward populations that can be documented, which reduces the resourcing available to address the hardest cases, which increases pressure on the shelters that still try. The diagram made the structural nature of the trap visible.

Empathy map walkthrough

The empathy map, developed after our interview with Matt, surfaced the lived experiences, frustrations, and unmet needs of a young adult experiencing homelessness in Edmonton. The persona thinks "I don't have any other choice right now" and "even if I try, I keep getting blocked (e.g., no ID)." The persona feels unsafe, dehumanized, overwhelmed by service complexity, and frustrated by lack of progress. The persona hears "you need photo ID to access this service" and "you don't qualify for this program" repeatedly across different organizations. The persona sees a system that is difficult to navigate, many different organizations that do not coordinate well, and other individuals struggling with similar issues.

Three insights surprised us most. First, homelessness is rarely a choice; most individuals feel they have no viable alternatives. Second, lack of photo identification is a major systemic barrier that prevents individuals from accessing even basic services. Third, many support organizations operate independently, resulting in poor coordination and individuals falling through gaps in the system.

Gold Mine Matrix

After the leverage analysis identified identification as the concrete, addressable barrier inside service fragmentation, the group ran a structured value-versus-challenge matrix on 22 candidate interventions. The Gold Mine quadrant (high value, low challenge) included the shelter-network photo ID card with QR code, which became the prototype direction. The matrix is what connects the systems analysis on this page to the prototype on the next: it is where the diagrams stopped being analysis and started pointing at something we could build.
