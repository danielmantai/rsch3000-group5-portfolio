Systemic Design Artifacts

Every diagram the group produced across Weeks 3 through 14, plus the walkthroughs that explain them.

How we used systemic design

The iceberg model, causal loop diagram, empathy map, and future-state system map each gave us a different lens on the same problem. Together they moved the group from visible events (encampments, shelter overcrowding) down through patterns, structures, and mental models, and then back up toward a proposed future state. Each artifact below is presented as a high-resolution image followed by a short walkthrough of what it surfaced.

Iceberg model walkthrough

The iceberg helped our team move beyond visible events such as shelter overcrowding and encampments to examine deeper patterns, structures, and mental models influencing homelessness in Edmonton. At the surface, we mapped the events most residents see: encampments in the river valley, shelter lineups, hospital emergency department pressure. One layer down, patterns: people cycling repeatedly through emergency services, losing identification, restarting intake from zero. Deeper still, structures: fragmented organizations, funding tied to bed counts rather than outcomes, and identification requirements embedded in funding accountability rules.

At the base of the iceberg we identified "housing as a commodity" as the mental model holding the rest of the structure in place. Policies, programs, and funding formulas all rest on that assumption. Shifting toward "housing as a right" would ripple through every layer above it.

Causal loop walkthrough

Our causal loop diagram identified reinforcing cycles around service strain, repeated displacement, and barriers to stable housing. The strongest reinforcing loop runs through identification: a person loses ID, cannot re-enter a funded program without ID, cannot get housing without the program, cannot get stable mailing address without housing, cannot replace ID without a mailing address. The loop closes on itself.

A second reinforcing loop runs through funding. When shelters cannot document service delivery for people without ID, those individuals become invisible to funders. Funding shifts toward populations that can be documented, which reduces the resourcing available to address the hardest cases, which increases pressure on the shelters that still try. The diagram made the structural nature of the trap visible.

Empathy map walkthrough

The empathy map, developed after our interview with Matt McMechan, surfaced the lived experiences, frustrations, and unmet needs of a young adult experiencing homelessness in Edmonton. The persona thinks "I don't have any other choice right now" and "even if I try, I keep getting blocked (e.g., no ID)." The persona feels unsafe, dehumanized, overwhelmed by service complexity, and frustrated by lack of progress. The persona hears "you need photo ID to access this service" and "you don't qualify for this program" repeatedly across different organizations. The persona sees a system that is difficult to navigate, many different organizations that do not coordinate well, and other individuals struggling with similar issues.

Three insights surfaced most surprised us. First, homelessness is rarely a choice; most individuals feel they have no viable alternatives. Second, lack of photo identification is a major systemic barrier that prevents individuals from accessing even basic services. Third, many support organizations operate independently, resulting in poor coordination and individuals falling through gaps in the system.

Future-state system map (Week 14 bonus)

The future-state map visualizes a coordinated Edmonton service network where identity is built through first contact rather than demanded at the door. Shelters and outreach teams issue ESN cards on-site, digital profiles persist across organizations, and funding claims are tied to documented service engagement rather than government-issued ID.
