Chapter 05

Business Model Canvas

How ESN Connect sustains itself at scale: partners, activities, resources, and the reinforcing loops that drive growth.

Value proposition

Enable unhoused individuals to build and maintain a verifiable identity within the service system, removing the need for government-issued ID before they can access support. The value works at three levels. For individuals, it means continuity: a lost card gets reprinted in minutes because the record is digital, breaking the cycle of obtaining, losing, and re-obtaining ID. For service providers, it cuts duplication by replacing five separate intakes with one shared record. For the system, it creates verifiable service records that satisfy Reaching Home funding requirements, making visible the people who are currently invisible to funders.

Key partners

Shelters and emergency housing providers are the entry point; without their buy-in, there is no system. Street outreach teams extend issuance to encampments and transit corridors. Homeward Trust Edmonton is the natural governance body, already coordinating Reaching Home funding across the city. Service Alberta is the long-term integration partner for bridging ESN records into the government ID process. Alberta Health Services and municipal/provincial housing policy offices round out the network where ID requirements currently block access.

Key activities

Onboarding shelters into the network, training frontline workers on card issuance and QR scanning, maintaining the digital platform, and establishing data governance protocols. The longer-term activity is policy advocacy: pushing for recognition of network-issued identity within funding frameworks so ESN Connect moves from a coordination tool to a structural intervention.

Key resources

The system builds on what shelters already have: intake staff, intake processes, and smartphones. Added resources are a secure cloud database (meeting Alberta FOIP requirements), low-cost card printers at each site, and the policy language from Housing First principles and Reaching Home directives.

Client relationships

The core relationship is between individuals and frontline workers: trust-based, in-person identity creation at first contact. Card issuance has to feel like help, not paperwork. Between providers, shared digital records replace repeated intake so clients are not forced to retell their story. Between providers and funders, ESN records supply the documentation needed to justify funding for people without government ID.

Client touchpoints

A physical laminated card issued on-site, a QR code linking to the digital profile, the ESN Connect website (client lookup for workers, service directory for individuals, funding explainer for policy audiences), and community kiosks at libraries and drop-in centres.

Client segments

Individuals experiencing homelessness without government ID. Frontline shelter and outreach workers. Service coordinators managing cross-organization referrals. Funders and government agencies that require documented service delivery. Municipal and provincial policy-makers working on housing strategy.

Systemic considerations

Two reinforcing loops drive growth. First, as more organizations adopt the system, more service records are created, which strengthens funding applications, which attracts more organizations. Second, as individuals build continuous service records, transitions to stable housing speed up, freeing shelter capacity for new clients.

Two balancing loops pose risks. If data governance is weak, a privacy breach erodes trust across the entire network. If the ID system is perceived as surveillance rather than support, individuals will avoid it. Client control over who sees their record is not a feature; it is a requirement.

The broader shift: identity moves from a gatekeeper at the door to something built through engagement. The goal is not a permanent parallel ID system. Success means ESN Connect demonstrates enough value that policy-makers simplify the government ID process for people in shelters.
